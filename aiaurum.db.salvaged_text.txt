**Power Factor:** The power factor is a measure of how effectively electrical power is being converted into useful work output. It is the ratio of active power (real power that does the work) to apparent power (the product of current and voltage in an AC circuit) and ranges from 0 to 1. A power factor closer to 1 indicates efficient utilization of electrical power.
**Transformer Losses:** Transformer losses primarily consist of two types: core losses (hysteresis and eddy current losses) that occur in the transformer's magnetic core, and copper losses that occur due to resistance in the windings when current flows through them. Efficient design minimizes these losses.
**Reactive Power:** Reactive power is the energy that oscillates between the source and load in an AC circuit, primarily used to establish and maintain electric and magnetic fields in inductive loads, like motors and transformers. It does not perform useful work but is essential for the functioning of AC systems.
**Difference between MCB and MCCB:** An MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker) is used for overcurrent protection and can automatically switch off electrical circuits in case of overloads or short circuits. An MCCB (Molded Case Circuit Breaker) offers similar protection but is used for higher voltage and current ratings, making it suitable for medium to large installations.
**How a Solar Inverter Works:** A solar inverter converts direct current (DC) generated by solar panels into alternating current (AC) that can be used by household appliances or fed into the electrical grid.
